Unit 3 Step 1
Purpose and Form of the Curriculum
In this unit we explore what the curriculum is , what helps to shape curricula, curricula for the future and to look at your own curriculum.
Learning Outcome
Analyse and articulate a developed understanding of the purposes, forms and impact of Local Curricula.
Activity
Watch the following video lecture and make notes below. This video introduces you to the concept of curricula.  It includes:
Defining curriculum.
What is education for?
How does a curriculum tell us what education is for? History, religion and politics.
Example of the development of the UK NC
Pisa, Timms and their impact on curricula.
Hidden curricula.
The link between curricula and views on learning.
Your notes will be helpful to the rest of this unit and further units.
Consider the following in your own school or setting and comment below:
•What subjects are most important?
•What is more important skills or knowledge?
•What do you think your curriculum says about the purpose of education?
•Are creative subjects valued?

Content Defining curriculum What is education for? How does a curriculum tell us what education is for? Example of the development of the UK NC Pisa, Timms and their impact on curricula Hidden curricula The link between curricula and views on learning.

Curriculum- A Highly Contested Term A school’s curriculum involves a complex interplay of beliefs, attitudes, skills, knowledge and understanding (Lewis and Ansell, 2010) All learning and other experiences that each school plans for its Pupils ( DfEE , 2000)

What is Education for? Education for passing Exams – ‘opening gateways to particular futures’ Education for preparation for life – skills for employment, contributing to and being a valued member of society. Education for 21 st century competing in the global economy Education for global awareness and change for the good

http://www.oecd.org/pisa/ https://schoolsweek.co.uk/pisa-2018-maths-up-no-improvement-in-reading-or-science/ Education for 21 st century competing in the global economy

Education for global awareness and change for the good

Forms of Curricula The curriculum includes the formal curriculum informal curriculum hidden curriculum omitted curriculum (Haydon and Helibronn , 2016)

UK Example Before 1988 Teaching was sporadic and inconsistent Too much freedom Inconsistencies

Formal Curriculum/Purpose of Education in the UK The curriculum will change according to the view of the current UK government on the purposes of education. One view is a belief of education as a body of knowledge to be transmitted ‘“I believe very strongly that education is about the transfer of knowledge from one generation to the next” (Gibb, 2010)

1944: Education Act and tripartite system; 1960s &amp; 1970s: comprehensive system; 1988: Education Reform Act National Curriculum; 2000: Curriculum 2000 2007: National Curriculum revised 2013: National Curriculum revised 2014: National Curriculum KS3 and KS4 updated 2015: Curriculum reform - GCSE and GCE 2015-2018 New GCSE and A level specifications introduced and first examinations. IB International Baccalaureate The Primary Years Programme : ( PYP – Kindergarten to Class 5) The Middle Years Programme : (MYP – Class 6 to Class 10) The Diploma Programme : ( DP – Class 11 to Class 12) Designed in 1968 in Geneva in response to a lack of flexibility in many schools across the world. It attempts to focus on real world situations, holistic education including empathy, critical thinking and problem solving, and is internationally recognised. Tightly controlled- school is assessed when applying to use the curriculum

Your Curriculum you work within. Think for 5 Minutes What subjects are most important? What is more important skills or knowledge? What do you think your curriculum says about the purpose of education? Are creative subjects valued?

Informal Curriculum- Preparation for life? These can be a major part of school lie and dominate the ethos Clubs and after school activities Sports Trips and outings Break times- equipment/ access to library etc School performances and exhibitions Life skills Personal and Social Education Worship and religious festivals

Hidden Curriculum ‘I shall take the term curriculum to comprise most of what children learn in school, including what is sometimes called the hidden curriculum, that is, the values and patterns of behaviour that are acquired, often incidentally …’ (Wragg, 1997, p.1) Reference list. Wragg, E. (1997) The Cubic Curriculum. London: Routledge.

Hidden Curriculum Uniforms Expectations Rules Attitudes to Pupils Punishment Awards for what? Food and eating Toilets! Pupil voice- school councils etc What content is covered in history, RE, politics etc.

Omitted Curriculum- could be… Black History! The worse sides of the British Empire Certain part of sex education Different religious points of view Climate change Opportunities related to gender stereotypes Freedom of expression

Recap Curriculum reflects views on the purposes of education It is influences by economic, political , religious and cultural views Some parts of the curriculum may not be explicit or even omitted. The next activities asks you to analyse the curriculum and the curriculum organisation in your setting. The reading offers a different view of a curriculum for future adults. Lastly you are asked to reflect on how much your values align with those of the curriculum you work within.

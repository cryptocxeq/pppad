Generative AI
Generative AI (Artificial Intelligence) has many applications and can be a useful a tool for your studies. However, you need to be careful when using AI for your assessment. If you do not reference or attribute AI content appropriately, this could be seen as an assessment offence. For further information speak to your tutor about the appropriate use of AI in specific assessments.
Here are two UWE Bristol guides to using AI in your studies and assessment:
This guide suggests ways to use AI effectively in your studies:
Generative AI study skills guide - Advice and guidance on how to make the most of generative artificial intelligence (AI).
This guide is about using AI in your assessment - including how to attribute and reference AI content appropriately to avoid assessment offences:
Using generative AI at UWE Bristol - A guide to when and how to use generative AI.
You are permitted to use Gen AI tools in an assistive role in this assessment to support you with ​
Editing original content and structuring original content;​
supporting a particular process such as translating content;​
giving your feedback on your clarity, coherence and level of criticality.​
Please note that while GenAI tools might suggest topics/content, you will always need to go back and locate and critically engage with actual literature/research/evidence yourself. ​
As the use of Gen AI is optional (due to its purely supportive capacity) it’s use is not part of the skills or knowledge you need to demonstrate for this assessment. However, if you have used Gen AI to prepare or produce part or all of your work, you must acknowledge its use. For more advice on this (and its use in general) please see your Generative AI study skills guide.​
Maintaining Your Academic Integrity and Avoiding Assessment Offences
Maintaining Your Academic Integrity and Avoiding Assessment Offences
Good academic practice is crucial to having academic integrity and avoiding assessment offences. You need to have the skills to produce work that show that you have understood, that you can use the ideas and work of other people appropriately and can think independently and develop your own argument. You can access the helpful resources for supporting academic integrity here.
Linked to this, it is important to understand the Academic Conduct Policy and Academic Misconduct Policy (which your teaching team can support with). Assessment Offences include, but are not limited to:
plagiarism: copying content from another source without using quotation marks even if the source is cited
plagiarism: copying content from another source without citing the author, even if words are changed
collusion: working with other current or previous students on individual submissions, or sharing your work with others thus allowing them to copy it
contract cheating: purchasing or obtaining work from another source
falsification or fabrication of content/ data
Where an assessment offence has been upheld this may result in a penalty that means you are required to repeat the assessment, this in turn can impact on progression and delay graduation. Not all assessment offences are intentional, a lack of intent does not excuse an assessment offence. If an assessment offence is alleged against you, you are encouraged to engage in the process to gain a clear understanding of the nature of the concerns and advice on what actions you should take to avoid a further allegation on a future piece of work.
